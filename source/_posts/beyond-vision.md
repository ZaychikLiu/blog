### 1. Do Ego and Taste Still Matter?

I have long felt that, in the current era, having strong taste and ego, or simply having one's own ideas, may be a bad thing for newcomers.

<!-- more -->

Today, if you want to work in AI, whether for graduate recommendation or PhD applications, any undergraduate who asks around will quickly learn that one is expected to join a lab in the first or second year and start publishing papers. It is no longer rare for juniors to already have one or two first-author top-tier conference papers. Three or four years ago, that would have been almost unimaginable.

Meanwhile, especially in China, many university research groups increasingly resemble corporate management structures. Projects are run by large coordinated teams and produced through pipelines, leaving fewer opportunities to truly train newcomers, while the resources still do not match those of companies. Companies, in contrast, have abundant resources, but they are not responsible for educating beginners. Who, then, is supposed to train newcomers? I have thought about this for a long time and still do not have an answer.

Along with various external pressures, students feel increasingly anxious about not being competitive. As a result, undergraduates and master's students are publishing more papers, and earlier than before, while the number of top-conference papers keeps expanding. At the same time, the LLM era has made research more capital-intensive, and academic labs often lack the resources needed for experiments. Research topics therefore become highly concentrated around a few supposedly publication-friendly directions.

This concentration means that almost every candidate competes on the same few tracks, and individual resumes become harder and harder to distinguish. Since most students do not have much beyond papers and internships, such as strong connections, the competition turns into a numbers game: more papers, more internships, more badges. But human energy is limited. Among these impressive-looking numbers, how much has truly been internalized? How much of the work actually shows taste?

We see too much territory-claiming work: someone builds a quick baseline around a new concept, and everyone after that has to follow it. We see too many benchmarks and surveys. Benchmarks are important as capability tests, of course, but most of them still fail to reflect real problems. We also see too many people chasing novelty for novelty's sake, producing work that feels strange more than meaningful.

For reasons like these, papers keep increasing in number, but they also become more homogeneous. Truly exciting work feels rarer. I used to be enthusiastic about reading papers, but now I find that even if I do not read papers for four months, it often does not seem to matter much. There is little real progress, and some new things feel oddly contrived.

I have begun to wonder how much foundational research is still left, at least in vision. In my view, the few foundational works that remain often come from engineering ideas transferred from LLMs. Vision itself seems to have very little that feels genuinely exciting.

In an era dominated by scaling up, we also have to think carefully about whether a setting is reasonable. I have heard people say that a method that cannot scale is not real research. I disagree. But if you propose a method, especially in a popular direction, you should state your setting clearly, because the method will probably not work at large scale, and different scales may even lead to completely different conclusions. Whether the system is well trained matters too.

After considering all these factors, and sometimes after finishing the experiments, one may find that almost no setting can withstand serious scrutiny. The method may not really work in a rigorous sense. Most of the gain comes from data; as long as the method does not hurt performance, it can be packaged into a paper. If there is not much left to change in the architecture, why not just build benchmarks and datasets directly?

I believe anyone who has done even a little research may feel depressed by this. Many times, you have an ego and an idea, you excitedly try something, and only later discover that industry has already tested the same thing and found it ineffective. They never published it, but your paper becomes a leftover fragment of an industrial failure, and industry verified it in one-tenth of the time. It is easy to fall into a kind of nihilism.

At the same time, the people you might think have no ego or taste have already published many papers, secured PhD offers, and found internships by following the standard path. Industry does not necessarily need a new paradigm anyway. Meanwhile, you look as if you have achieved nothing, and may not even be able to publish your paper.

Everyone knows that papers are less useful than before. What you are doing may already have been done. Your SOTA may last only a week. Your submitted paper may be ignored once the hot topic disappears. Yet the current evaluation system still demands papers.

If everyone knows that something big will eventually happen in the distant future, but it is impossible at the time and will not depend on any single person's breakthrough, what should we think of someone who waits until it happens and then says, "I was right," taking credit after the fact? Is that admirable? Is that persistence? They can indeed say they anticipated the future. Does that mean their research taste was good? I find it hard to judge.

So I now feel that in a hot field like AI, before ordinary students become established, idealism may have little room to survive. Perhaps they should not have too much long-termism, ego, or taste too early. People have to survive first before they earn the right to discuss such things. When younger students ask me for advice, I often tell them to publish a few papers first, follow hot directions, do what senior people ask, and secure their position. In the eyes of seniors, being reliable matters more than being imaginative. If you refuse to follow and have no endorsement, you are likely to be the one who loses most badly in this prisoner's dilemma.

Taste without resources, endorsement, or a paper buffer is risky. Revealing your taste and ego too early can easily be punished by the system. The current evaluation system does not reward judgment itself. It rewards visible output, verifiable credentials, and stable execution. After a field becomes stable in a certain sense, it no longer needs critics. I find this very sad. I believe few students who truly love research want to see or participate in this, but perhaps there are not many choices.

Of course, from another angle, a person with enough ego and taste may be confident enough not to care whether these things matter. Under a different evaluation system, we might reach the opposite conclusion. We never wanted to become machines. If we have no taste and no ego, how are we different from machines? Even without top-tier taste in a worldly sense, or without conventional achievements, living and doing research in the way one hopes to is already a kind of happiness. Some ideas that now seem impossible may one day become common. I deeply admire people who can remain internally coherent. At least they seem far better off than me, with my daily self-doubt.

### 2. Stay in Academia or Move to Industry?

Since 2025 and 2026, almost all the news I have heard has been unfavorable to academic research. At least in North America, it has become increasingly common for PhD students to quit, master out, graduate in three years and immediately join industry, or for professors to go on leave to start companies or join frontier labs. Almost every US PhD student I know in LLM-related areas is considering industry, sometimes even giving up the degree. Many people who had not yet started PhD programs have also declined offers and gone directly from undergraduate or master's programs into frontier labs in China or abroad.

I used to have role models who stayed with 3DV or vision-centric research. But I have found that the ones doing best now tend to be those who moved fastest into LLMs, MLLMs, video generation, or robotics, and then quickly joined industry. It feels as if any PhD even slightly connected to LLMs must now seriously consider quitting, mastering out, or going on leave. Three years ago this would have been hard to imagine. I only hope that when I apply in the future, there will still be professors in my field who remain in academia.

2026 feels like an extremely strange year. If we speak bluntly about compensation, PhD-level offers of 2 to 3 million RMB in China no longer seem rare, while the US market is even more extreme, with places like Thinking Machines reportedly averaging above 10 million dollars. Winners take all. At the same time, everyone knows that for PhD applications, unless one has very strong connections, the bar for North America is approaching what used to be the level of a graduating PhD. Many current undergraduates now have almost unbelievable backgrounds. Those with connections often come from specific schools or even specific elite programs.

At the same time, almost everyone says that, at least for LLMs, this may be the last year to get on the train. New graduates in North America are already finding it hard to get positions, partly because frontier labs are heading toward IPOs and hiring becomes more constrained. People are also gradually realizing that the technical stacks across frontier labs may be quite similar. No one seems to hold an absolute technical advantage. The architectures appear to cycle through a few familiar patterns. Traditional algorithmic innovation may no longer be necessary, and new algorithms may even make the whole system worse.

The key to building good models may now lie more in data and infrastructure. On the human side, it may depend on organizational structure and minimizing political friction. Before 2025, many people looked for research internships that offered free exploration, hoping to do something interesting and new. Some even looked down on engineering-heavy work as dirty labor. Now the tone has shifted: "Could you take me through data cleaning so I can deeply understand what data a model needs?" or "Could you teach me how to handle communication and infrastructure at large scale, so I can understand MoE tuning?"

People no longer seem to chase novelty. Instead, everyone rushes to claim a place inside foundation model teams. Pretraining comes first. If that fails, try post-training. Algorithm work, excluding infrastructure, is best. If that is unavailable, do infrastructure. If there is no algorithm work, do data and benchmarks. If even data and benchmark work has no model access, then simply survive. If nothing else works, join a product team that trains models and repeat the same process.

In the end, many people realize that the work looks similar everywhere, the structures look similar everywhere, and the technical components are already mature. Then why keep studying? If there is a chance to claim a position, take it first. This seems quite different from the algorithm roles we once imagined. Many people may start doubting their lives. If I put the energy required for PhD applications and fellowships into job hunting instead, then at least my papers would not only exist as expected value four or five years in the future. I could convert effort into outcomes quickly. Objectively speaking, most people probably do not have a pure research ideal. They may simply think this field pays well, and that a PhD has a higher ceiling or a higher floor. Life still has to be considered.

Everyone is also anxious because agents are becoming so strong. Much of research is labor-intensive. If automated research becomes mature in one or two years, will we still need humans to do research? Why must human taste be better than an agent's, especially when agents can access more and more context? Industry research scientists were once compared to academic PIs. But now faculty hiring and tenure pressure are intense, and the number of PhDs keeps rising. Who knows what things will look like in five years? Are today's research scientist roles really immune to the pressures that now affect the PI system?

Some may ask: do we no longer need newcomers? But at present, the cost of people may be much lower than the cost of compute and APIs. When an intern can access hundreds of GPUs while earning barely over 10,000 RMB per month, and the electricity cost alone can easily exceed that several times, is the person using the GPUs, or are the GPUs using the person? When some industry teams give each person a weekly API budget of 250,000 dollars, is the person the agent, or is the person controlling the agent?

If newcomers are truly needed, the market will find a way to train them. If the market is unwilling to train newcomers, it means the cost of doing so is much higher than the benefit. Fundamentally, training models does not require that many people, at least far fewer than the number of newcomers in the market. This is a matter of supply and demand. Many people may simply be lifted by the trend rather than by personal ability. When the door finally closes, the result may be that there are no newcomers left, because newcomers have no resources, cannot do training, and have nowhere to go.

Academia should probably not compete with industry on directions that industry has almost solved, because it cannot really compete. But what about new tasks, or directions where things still do not work? Those should have been places where academia continues to innovate. Yet with the enormous salaries industry now offers AI talent, how many people can give up a guaranteed package to pursue a high-risk new direction? Much innovative work eventually becomes just one component on a machine inside a large company. Many PhD students' representative works are produced during industry internships rather than in academia. And if something does not work in industry, especially at scale, should academia really be expected to solve it?

Academia also rarely has enough resources to solve such problems. If one wants both resources and research opportunities, large companies already have their own research departments. Why must the work be done in academia? Some people may say academia should focus on new concepts and new routes. But if no one endorses you, almost nobody cares. Is that not also a kind of privilege?

So perhaps we need a reason to stay. For now, academia can still provide an identity of freedom. But this freedom also seems to be increasingly constrained by external forces.
