    <h3>1. Ego和Taste还重要吗</h3>
    <p>我其实一直以来认为，在当下这个时代，对于新人而言，拥有好的taste和ego，或者说有自己的想法，可能是一件坏事。</p>
    <p>目前只要你想要做AI这个领域，保研或者申请PhD，如果稍微打听下，本科生都知道要在大一大二就开始进组，然后开始发论文，而且现在到了大三有1-2篇A会一作的学生已经不在少数了，这放到3-4年前完全不敢想象。</p>
    <p>并且现在，尤其是在国内，我们会发现很多校内课题组，好像越来越像企业管理架构了，做很多项目都是集团军作战，流水线产出，很难遇到培养新人的机会，但是资源却并没有对标企业。与之相对的是企业，他们资源丰富，但并不承担培养新人的责任。那新人谁来培养呢？我想了很久并没有得到答案。</p>
    <p>同时伴随着各种外部因素，学生各种压力越来越大，也越来越恐惧自己无法竞争，于是我们会发现这么一类事情：本科生/硕士生发文章越来越多、越来最早，顶会数量越来越膨胀。但同时由于LLM时代重资产化，学术界的资源越来越不够做实验，所以大家的方向高度集中在那么几个所谓“好发文章”的方向。</p>
    <p>这样主题的高度集中，又导致所有的candidate几乎都是在少数几个赛道竞争，然后个体简历之间越来越没有区别。但是大部分学生并没有论文/实习之外的一些东西，也就是所谓的connection，所以大家又开始比数量，刷论文数量，集邮式实习，但是人的精力终究有限，这些看似光鲜亮丽的数量里，有多少是自己沉淀的呢？又或者说，有多少工作是有taste的呢？</p>
    <p>我们看到太多占坑式工作，在新概念上先做一个baseline，但是不做又不行，后面的人都得follow你；我们看到太多Benchmark和Survey，Benchmark作为能力测试自然非常重要，但是不妨碍绝大多数Benchmark都不能反映真实问题；我们看到太多人为了novel而novel，最后做出来一些非常猎奇的作品。</p>
    <p>诸如此类原因，我们会发现文章越来越多，但是同质化越来越严重，令人excited的工作越来越少，我之前很热衷于看文章，但现在发现我4个月不看好像也没关系，基本没有进展，有些新东西反而非常猎奇。</p>
    <p>我已经开始怀疑，至少在Vision这个领域，还剩下多少foundation research？至少我认为这少数foundation的work，几乎都是来源于LLM那边的工程迁移，实际上Vision本身几乎没有什么很exciting的新东西了。</p>
    <p>同时我们会发现，在scaling up横行的时代，我们确实需要考虑setting的合理性。我其实听到很多说法是，“不能scale的method就不是真科研”，我是反对的；但是你如果要做一个method，尤其是热门方向，你确实应该claim自己的setting，因为这一套大概率在large scale上不work，甚至不同scale上会有完全不同的结论，还涉及到有没有well trained。</p>
    <p>种种因素考虑完，甚至做完实验发现，其实几乎没有什么setting是能经得起推敲的，method严谨来看不也work，大部分收益来源于数据，method不掉点只能包装下发文章，反正架构也没啥好改的，那为啥不直接做benchmark造数据得了？</p>
    <p>我相信可能真正做过一点research的人都会感到depressed。甚至很多时候我们会发现，你有自己的Ego，有想法去做一些尝试，兴高采烈地做完。但是后面发现，业界早就试过你的做法，并且得到了否定的结论。他们没发出来，但你的文章只是业界失败的边角料，并且业界验证的时间是你的十分之一，那我们就会陷入某种虚无主义。</p>
    <p>与此同时，那些被你评价为没有Ego和Taste的人，他们已经按部就班发了很多文章，申请到了PhD，找到了实习，反正业界也不需要什么新范式，而你却看起来一事无成，甚至文章都发不出去。</p>
    <p>大家都知道现在paper没用，你做的东西很可能已经被做过，你的sota仅限于1周内，你的文章投出去也因为热点消失而无人问津，但目前的评价体系还是要paper。</p>
    <p>如果大家都知道一个很大的东西很久的未来会实现，但是当时完全不可能实现，并且不会依赖于任何一个人的突破，而有人却在它实现之后，反过头来说“我是对的”，然后拿走了credit，这种行为真的值得被欣赏吗？这算是坚持吗？他们确实也可以说是预料到了未来发展，那是他们research taste够好吗？我很难评价。</p>
    <p>所以我现在觉得在AI这个热门行业，普通学生在成名前，理想主义可能没有什么生存空间，可能也不太应该有长期主义，也不应该有太强的Ego和Taste，大家得先活下去，才有资格讨论。我现在推荐学弟学妹，先发几篇，追逐热门方向，大哥说什么你就做什么，保障生存，在senior眼中“靠谱”比“有想法”更重要。你不follow，并且你也没有背书的话，你大概率就是“囚徒博弈”中输的最惨的那一类。</p>
    <p>没有资源、背书、paper buffer 的 taste，过早暴露你的taste和ego，是风险，很容易被系统惩罚。当前评价体系并不奖励“判断力”本身，它更奖励可见产出、可验证履历和执行稳定性，毕竟这个行业在某种意义上稳定后，不需要评论家。我对这种现象感到非常悲伤，我相信其实也没有热爱research的同学愿意看到或者参与，但是可能实在没有太多选择的余地。</p>
    <p>当然，换个角度看，一个ego且taste足够好的人完全可以自信到不在意这个东西重不重要，如果换一个评价体系，可能我们可以得到完全相反的结论，我们从来不想做机器，如果没有taste和ego，那我们和机器有什么区别呢？即使没有世俗意义上的顶尖taste/ago，或者成功学的成绩，也依然是按自己希冀的方式过生活/做研究，本身就是一种幸福。而且现在看起来可能天方夜谭的一些idea，可能大家觉得它未来也不可能实现，有没有可能真的普及呢？我其实非常佩服一些能自洽的朋友，至少比我每日内耗好太多了。</p>
    <h3>2. 坚守学界还是投奔业界</h3>
    <p>其实25-26年以来，我基本听到的都是不利于学界Research的消息。一方面是，至少在北美，PhD quit/Master out/三年毕业然后光速加入业界，或者是Professor on leave去创业/加入Frontier Lab，这种事情已经屡见不鲜了，甚至愈演愈烈。我认识的几乎所有和LLM相关领域的US PhD，都在考虑去业界，学位也不要了，然后没入学PhD的也有很多放弃了offer，选择直接本科/硕士直接进入国内外的frontier lab。</p>
    <p>我之前也曾经有过一些坚守偶像，比如3DV的或者Vision Centric的，我发现他们当中目前混的最好的，和转行做LLM/MLLM/Video Gen/Robotics的速度成正比，然后也是光速加入业界。感觉现在但凡是个PhD，只要稍微和LLM沾点边的，都要把quit/ms out/on leave严肃纳入考虑。这放到3年前是非常难以想象的事情，我希望我未来可能申请的时候，还能剩下领域内的Professor在坚守学界。</p>
    <p>2026年感觉是非常疯狂的一年，如果让我们功利一点数大包，国内phd给到2-3M rmb似乎已经不罕见，美国则更加疯狂，甚至还有TBD这种average 10M dollars往上的存在，winners get all。同时目前的PhD申请，基本上人人都知道，没有特别强conn的，申请北美几乎Bar要等于往年PhD毕业的水平，现在的很多本科生，已经bg恐怖到了匪夷所思的地步；而有conn的，也基本上专属于某些学校甚至是特色班。</p>
    <p>但与此同时，几乎所有人都在说，至少在LLM，可能今年就是上车的最后一年，北美的NG已经开始因为Frontier Lab要上市而很难找到一个position了。 并且现在大家可能慢慢发现，好像各个Frontier Lab的技术栈其实都差不多，没有人手握什么绝对优势技术，大家的架构好像来来回回就是那么几样，传统意义上的算法似乎并不需要创新，甚至新算法可能对于整个系统会更糟糕。</p>
    <p>能不能做好模型，技术上的关键可能更在于数据和Infra，人的层面看则是组织架构与尽可能少的政治内斗。我记得25年以前，大家找实习都是希望找一段“自由探索”的reserach intern，做点好玩的新东西，并且有点看不起一些工程化的所谓“脏活累活”；但是现在大家的风向逐渐转为，“哥，你可以带我洗一遍数据吗，我想深刻领悟下模型需要什么数据”，或者，“哥，你可以带我怎么在large scale上玩通信，写infra吗，我想领悟MoE的调参”。</p>
    <p>大家好像不再追求什么新东西，而是争先恐后地，想要在基模组里面占坑：Pretrain优先，不行就尝试Post Train；算法（除infra外）最好，不行就尝试去做infra；如果算法都没得做，那就去做data和benchmark；如果做data benchmark连model都没法access，那还是苟住；实在不行咱们就去业务组找个训模型的地方，重复上面的流程。</p>
    <p>结果到最后，我知道有很多人，发现好像哪里做的事情都差不多，哪里的结构都差不多，而且技术上都是已有成熟的，那还读啥书，有机会占坑就先去占着，这好像和我们认知中的算法岗差的有点多啊！估计很多人会怀疑人生，我如果把申请PhD xxx Fellowship的力气拿去找工作，那至少我的paper之类就不是仅存在于4-5年后预期，我可以迅速变现。至少客观来看，大部分人其实并没有什么科研理想，大家可能只是觉得这个好赚钱，读PhD可能上下限高，总是要考虑生活的。</p>
    <p>而且现在所有人都在焦虑，Agent这么强了，科研其实有很多都是劳动密集型，当Auto Research 1-2年内成熟了，我们真的还需要人来做科研吗？为什么人的Taste就一定会比Agent好，尤其是Agent现在能接触的context越来越多？当年业界的RS是对标学校的PI的，但是现在找教职以及Tenure压力这么大，PhD也越来越多，谁知道5年后会怎么样呢？现在的RS真的不会步了PI体系的后尘吗？</p>
    <p>可能也有人会反驳，那难道不需要新人了吗？但目前情况是，人的费用可能远比算力和API低，当一个实习生可以access到百卡，但是拿着堪堪过1w的工资，电费都可以轻松超过这个数几倍，那到底是人在跑卡，还是卡在跑人？当有的业界组每周API额度每人可以有25w美元，那到底人是Agent，还是人在操控Agent？</p>
    <p>如果缺新人，那么市场总会找到办法培养；如果市场不愿意养新人，说明这些新人培养的成本远大于收益。本质上，还是因为训模型本身不需要那么多人，至少远少于市场上的新人。这是个市场供需关系。大家可能都是在风口上飞，而不是自己能力问题。到最后“关车门”的结果就是没有新人了，因为新人根本没有resource，没法做training，哪都去不了。</p>
    <p>学界确实不应该，在业界已经almost solved的方向上和业界竞争，因为完全无法竞争。但New Tasks？或者一些没做work的方向？他们本应是academia继续创新的方向。然而, 在今天工业界为 AI 人才开出的巨额薪水下, 有几个人能放弃到手大包去尝试高风险的新方向? 做出的创新的工作, 最终大多也只是去大厂成为机器上的一环。很多PhD的代表作，不少是在业界实习时才做出来的吧，并非是学界。而且业界做不work的事情，尤其涉及scale的话，真的应该交给学界解决吗？</p>
    <p>而且学界也很难有足够的资源去解决，更何况如果要资源，又要有reseach机会，那大厂本来就有自己的研究部门，为何一定要在学界做呢？可能有些观点是，学界应该做“新观念”“新路线”，但是这些东西，如果没人为你背书，几乎nobody cares啊？这何尝不是一种privilege呢？</p>
    <p>所以我们可能需要一个坚守的理由，目前来看，学界能给我们提供一个“自由”的身份，但是这份“自由”似乎也随着外部的各种因素变得越来越有限了。</p>

    <h3>1. Do Ego and Taste Still Matter?</h3>
    <p>I have long felt that, in the current era, having strong taste and ego, or simply having one's own ideas, may be a bad thing for newcomers.</p>
    <p>Today, if you want to work in AI, whether for graduate recommendation or PhD applications, any undergraduate who asks around will quickly learn that one is expected to join a lab in the first or second year and start publishing papers. It is no longer rare for juniors to already have one or two first-author top-tier conference papers. Three or four years ago, that would have been almost unimaginable.</p>
    <p>Meanwhile, especially in China, many university research groups increasingly resemble corporate management structures. Projects are run by large coordinated teams and produced through pipelines, leaving fewer opportunities to truly train newcomers, while the resources still do not match those of companies. Companies, in contrast, have abundant resources, but they are not responsible for educating beginners. Who, then, is supposed to train newcomers? I have thought about this for a long time and still do not have an answer.</p>
    <p>Along with various external pressures, students feel increasingly anxious about not being competitive. As a result, undergraduates and master's students are publishing more papers, and earlier than before, while the number of top-conference papers keeps expanding. At the same time, the LLM era has made research more capital-intensive, and academic labs often lack the resources needed for experiments. Research topics therefore become highly concentrated around a few supposedly publication-friendly directions.</p>
    <p>This concentration means that almost every candidate competes on the same few tracks, and individual resumes become harder and harder to distinguish. Since most students do not have much beyond papers and internships, such as strong connections, the competition turns into a numbers game: more papers, more internships, more badges. But human energy is limited. Among these impressive-looking numbers, how much has truly been internalized? How much of the work actually shows taste?</p>
    <p>We see too much territory-claiming work: someone builds a quick baseline around a new concept, and everyone after that has to follow it. We see too many benchmarks and surveys. Benchmarks are important as capability tests, of course, but most of them still fail to reflect real problems. We also see too many people chasing novelty for novelty's sake, producing work that feels strange more than meaningful.</p>
    <p>For reasons like these, papers keep increasing in number, but they also become more homogeneous. Truly exciting work feels rarer. I used to be enthusiastic about reading papers, but now I find that even if I do not read papers for four months, it often does not seem to matter much. There is little real progress, and some new things feel oddly contrived.</p>
    <p>I have begun to wonder how much foundational research is still left, at least in vision. In my view, the few foundational works that remain often come from engineering ideas transferred from LLMs. Vision itself seems to have very little that feels genuinely exciting.</p>
    <p>In an era dominated by scaling up, we also have to think carefully about whether a setting is reasonable. I have heard people say that a method that cannot scale is not real research. I disagree. But if you propose a method, especially in a popular direction, you should state your setting clearly, because the method will probably not work at large scale, and different scales may even lead to completely different conclusions. Whether the system is well trained matters too.</p>
    <p>After considering all these factors, and sometimes after finishing the experiments, one may find that almost no setting can withstand serious scrutiny. The method may not really work in a rigorous sense. Most of the gain comes from data; as long as the method does not hurt performance, it can be packaged into a paper. If there is not much left to change in the architecture, why not just build benchmarks and datasets directly?</p>
    <p>I believe anyone who has done even a little research may feel depressed by this. Many times, you have an ego and an idea, you excitedly try something, and only later discover that industry has already tested the same thing and found it ineffective. They never published it, but your paper becomes a leftover fragment of an industrial failure, and industry verified it in one-tenth of the time. It is easy to fall into a kind of nihilism.</p>
    <p>At the same time, the people you might think have no ego or taste have already published many papers, secured PhD offers, and found internships by following the standard path. Industry does not necessarily need a new paradigm anyway. Meanwhile, you look as if you have achieved nothing, and may not even be able to publish your paper.</p>
    <p>Everyone knows that papers are less useful than before. What you are doing may already have been done. Your SOTA may last only a week. Your submitted paper may be ignored once the hot topic disappears. Yet the current evaluation system still demands papers.</p>
    <p>If everyone knows that something big will eventually happen in the distant future, but it is impossible at the time and will not depend on any single person's breakthrough, what should we think of someone who waits until it happens and then says, "I was right," taking credit after the fact? Is that admirable? Is that persistence? They can indeed say they anticipated the future. Does that mean their research taste was good? I find it hard to judge.</p>
    <p>So I now feel that in a hot field like AI, before ordinary students become established, idealism may have little room to survive. Perhaps they should not have too much long-termism, ego, or taste too early. People have to survive first before they earn the right to discuss such things. When younger students ask me for advice, I often tell them to publish a few papers first, follow hot directions, do what senior people ask, and secure their position. In the eyes of seniors, being reliable matters more than being imaginative. If you refuse to follow and have no endorsement, you are likely to be the one who loses most badly in this prisoner's dilemma.</p>
    <p>Taste without resources, endorsement, or a paper buffer is risky. Revealing your taste and ego too early can easily be punished by the system. The current evaluation system does not reward judgment itself. It rewards visible output, verifiable credentials, and stable execution. After a field becomes stable in a certain sense, it no longer needs critics. I find this very sad. I believe few students who truly love research want to see or participate in this, but perhaps there are not many choices.</p>
    <p>Of course, from another angle, a person with enough ego and taste may be confident enough not to care whether these things matter. Under a different evaluation system, we might reach the opposite conclusion. We never wanted to become machines. If we have no taste and no ego, how are we different from machines? Even without top-tier taste in a worldly sense, or without conventional achievements, living and doing research in the way one hopes to is already a kind of happiness. Some ideas that now seem impossible may one day become common. I deeply admire people who can remain internally coherent. At least they seem far better off than me, with my daily self-doubt.</p>
    <h3>2. Stay in Academia or Move to Industry?</h3>
    <p>Since 2025 and 2026, almost all the news I have heard has been unfavorable to academic research. At least in North America, it has become increasingly common for PhD students to quit, master out, graduate in three years and immediately join industry, or for professors to go on leave to start companies or join frontier labs. Almost every US PhD student I know in LLM-related areas is considering industry, sometimes even giving up the degree. Many people who had not yet started PhD programs have also declined offers and gone directly from undergraduate or master's programs into frontier labs in China or abroad.</p>
    <p>I used to have role models who stayed with 3DV or vision-centric research. But I have found that the ones doing best now tend to be those who moved fastest into LLMs, MLLMs, video generation, or robotics, and then quickly joined industry. It feels as if any PhD even slightly connected to LLMs must now seriously consider quitting, mastering out, or going on leave. Three years ago this would have been hard to imagine. I only hope that when I apply in the future, there will still be professors in my field who remain in academia.</p>
    <p>2026 feels like an extremely strange year. If we speak bluntly about compensation, PhD-level offers of 2 to 3 million RMB in China no longer seem rare, while the US market is even more extreme, with places like Thinking Machines reportedly averaging above 10 million dollars. Winners take all. At the same time, everyone knows that for PhD applications, unless one has very strong connections, the bar for North America is approaching what used to be the level of a graduating PhD. Many current undergraduates now have almost unbelievable backgrounds. Those with connections often come from specific schools or even specific elite programs.</p>
    <p>At the same time, almost everyone says that, at least for LLMs, this may be the last year to get on the train. New graduates in North America are already finding it hard to get positions, partly because frontier labs are heading toward IPOs and hiring becomes more constrained. People are also gradually realizing that the technical stacks across frontier labs may be quite similar. No one seems to hold an absolute technical advantage. The architectures appear to cycle through a few familiar patterns. Traditional algorithmic innovation may no longer be necessary, and new algorithms may even make the whole system worse.</p>
    <p>The key to building good models may now lie more in data and infrastructure. On the human side, it may depend on organizational structure and minimizing political friction. Before 2025, many people looked for research internships that offered free exploration, hoping to do something interesting and new. Some even looked down on engineering-heavy work as dirty labor. Now the tone has shifted: "Could you take me through data cleaning so I can deeply understand what data a model needs?" or "Could you teach me how to handle communication and infrastructure at large scale, so I can understand MoE tuning?"</p>
    <p>People no longer seem to chase novelty. Instead, everyone rushes to claim a place inside foundation model teams. Pretraining comes first. If that fails, try post-training. Algorithm work, excluding infrastructure, is best. If that is unavailable, do infrastructure. If there is no algorithm work, do data and benchmarks. If even data and benchmark work has no model access, then simply survive. If nothing else works, join a product team that trains models and repeat the same process.</p>
    <p>In the end, many people realize that the work looks similar everywhere, the structures look similar everywhere, and the technical components are already mature. Then why keep studying? If there is a chance to claim a position, take it first. This seems quite different from the algorithm roles we once imagined. Many people may start doubting their lives. If I put the energy required for PhD applications and fellowships into job hunting instead, then at least my papers would not only exist as expected value four or five years in the future. I could convert effort into outcomes quickly. Objectively speaking, most people probably do not have a pure research ideal. They may simply think this field pays well, and that a PhD has a higher ceiling or a higher floor. Life still has to be considered.</p>
    <p>Everyone is also anxious because agents are becoming so strong. Much of research is labor-intensive. If automated research becomes mature in one or two years, will we still need humans to do research? Why must human taste be better than an agent's, especially when agents can access more and more context? Industry research scientists were once compared to academic PIs. But now faculty hiring and tenure pressure are intense, and the number of PhDs keeps rising. Who knows what things will look like in five years? Are today's research scientist roles really immune to the pressures that now affect the PI system?</p>
    <p>Some may ask: do we no longer need newcomers? But at present, the cost of people may be much lower than the cost of compute and APIs. When an intern can access hundreds of GPUs while earning barely over 10,000 RMB per month, and the electricity cost alone can easily exceed that several times, is the person using the GPUs, or are the GPUs using the person? When some industry teams give each person a weekly API budget of 250,000 dollars, is the person the agent, or is the person controlling the agent?</p>
    <p>If newcomers are truly needed, the market will find a way to train them. If the market is unwilling to train newcomers, it means the cost of doing so is much higher than the benefit. Fundamentally, training models does not require that many people, at least far fewer than the number of newcomers in the market. This is a matter of supply and demand. Many people may simply be lifted by the trend rather than by personal ability. When the door finally closes, the result may be that there are no newcomers left, because newcomers have no resources, cannot do training, and have nowhere to go.</p>
    <p>Academia should probably not compete with industry on directions that industry has almost solved, because it cannot really compete. But what about new tasks, or directions where things still do not work? Those should have been places where academia continues to innovate. Yet with the enormous salaries industry now offers AI talent, how many people can give up a guaranteed package to pursue a high-risk new direction? Much innovative work eventually becomes just one component on a machine inside a large company. Many PhD students' representative works are produced during industry internships rather than in academia. And if something does not work in industry, especially at scale, should academia really be expected to solve it?</p>
    <p>Academia also rarely has enough resources to solve such problems. If one wants both resources and research opportunities, large companies already have their own research departments. Why must the work be done in academia? Some people may say academia should focus on new concepts and new routes. But if no one endorses you, almost nobody cares. Is that not also a kind of privilege?</p>
    <p>So perhaps we need a reason to stay. For now, academia can still provide an identity of freedom. But this freedom also seems to be increasingly constrained by external forces.</p>
